Hey again 👋

So you're shopping for a home EV charger — brilliant. But before you buy the first one that looks good on Amazon, there's something worth knowing:

**Most people buy the wrong charger for their setup.**

Not a "bad" charger, necessarily. Just one that doesn't fit how they actually live and charge. Here's what catches people out:

**🏠 House or flat?**
If you're in a flat or don't have a driveway, your options are different. Some chargers are designed specifically for shared parking or on-street solutions, while others assume you've got a garage wall to mount on. Getting this wrong means paying for an installation that doesn't work.

**⚡ Smart tariff compatibility**
This is the big one. If your charger can talk to your energy provider's smart tariff (like Octopus Intelligent Go), it can automatically charge your car during the cheapest overnight hours — as low as 3.5p per kWh. But not all chargers support this. If you're planning to use a smart tariff (and you should be), check compatibility *before* you buy.

**☀️ Solar panels?**
If you've got solar panels — or plan to get them — you'll want a charger with solar diverting. This lets the charger use your excess solar energy to charge your car for free during the day. Not every charger does this, and some do it much better than others.

**💷 Budget**
Decent chargers range from about £300 to £1,200+ before installation. The sweet spot for most people is £400–£700. Don't overspend on features you won't use, but don't go too cheap on something that'll be on your wall for the next decade.

**Subject:** How to get £350 off your EV charger installation
**Preview text:** The government grant most people forget to claim.

---

Quick one for you today — did you know the UK government will pay **up to £350** towards your home EV charger installation?

It's called the **OZEV Electric Vehicle Chargepoint Grant**, and a surprising number of people either don't know about it or assume they won't qualify.

**Here's the gist:**

- The grant covers **75% of the cost** of buying and installing a home chargepoint, up to **£350**
- You don't get the cash directly — your installer claims it on your behalf and knocks it off your bill
- Most standard installations (house with a driveway, off-street parking) qualify
- You need to use an **OZEV-approved installer** (we list them in our guide)

**Who qualifies?**

- You own or rent a property in the UK
- You have dedicated off-street parking
- You own, lease, or have ordered an eligible electric vehicle
- The charger is installed by an OZEV-approved installer

**Who doesn't qualify?**

- New-build homes (the developer should include a charger under separate building regulations)
- If you've already claimed the grant at the same property

It takes about 5 minutes to sort out and saves you up to £350. There's genuinely no reason not to.

### Email 4 — Day 10

**Subject:** The smart tariff secret most EV owners miss
**Preview text:** What if charging your car cost just 3.5p per kWh?

---

Here's a number that might surprise you:

**3.5p per kWh.**

That's what some UK EV owners are paying to charge their cars at home. Compare that to the average electricity rate of around 24p/kWh, and you can see why this matters.

The secret? **Smart EV tariffs.**

The most popular one is **Octopus Intelligent Go**. Here's how it works:

1. You sign up for the tariff with Octopus Energy
2. You connect your EV charger (it needs to be a compatible smart charger)
3. You tell the app what time you need your car ready by and how much charge you need
4. Octopus automatically shifts your charging to the cheapest overnight window — usually between midnight and 5:30am
5. You pay just **7.5p/kWh** for all electricity used during those hours (some compatible setups achieve effective rates even lower)

**What does that mean in real money?**

A typical UK EV (let's say a 60kWh battery) charging from 20% to 80% would cost roughly:
- **On a standard tariff (24p/kWh):** ~£8.64
- **On Octopus Intelligent Go (7.5p/kWh):** ~£2.70

Over a year, smart tariff charging can save you **£500–£800** compared to charging on a standard rate. That's not a typo.

**The catch?** Your charger needs to be compatible. Not all of them are. The Ohme Home Pro, for example, has excellent smart tariff integration. Some cheaper chargers don't support it at all.
